Resident Evil 9

Level Design Breakdown 03

Overview

This article focuses on how the game uses sightline design, color contrast, object placement, and route planning to guide the player through a tense interior sequence.

Breakdown

In this sequence, when the player opens the door, they immediately see a half-open door directly ahead, which draws attention and invites further inspection. As the player moves closer, a branching path appears on the right, where red lighting is used to attract attention. At this point, the player seems to have two choices: continue toward the half-open door or take the path on the right. However, no matter which option is chosen first, the player is ultimately led toward the right-hand path, since the half-open door reveals only darkness inside, and the player has no means of lighting the space.

The interesting point here is that the level creates a false choice, but the reason it cannot be taken feels believable. The player is not stopped by an arbitrary restriction, but by a condition that makes sense within the situation. Because of this, the guidance feels natural rather than forced.

When the player takes the path on the right, the corridor is mainly presented in red. At the same time, a white switch stands out and attracts the player’s attention, encouraging interaction. When the player activates it, the interaction angle makes the result clearly visible: the lights in the distant corridor turn on, showing where the player should go next.

What is effective here is first the use of color. The red corridor creates a sense of danger and unease, while also making the white-lit switch stand out more strongly. At the same time, the camera position during interaction ensures that the player can immediately see the result of their action, connecting the switch to the newly lit path ahead.

As the player continues forward, they encounter two doors. One requires a key to open, while the other is too dark to explore. An important design choice here is that the two doors are not mainly distinguished through their room numbers, 201 and 202, but through their visual appearance. In my view, this reflects a “recognition rather than recall” approach. After going through a stretch of exploration, the player may not remember which door number was locked and which one led into darkness, but they are more likely to remember that the door with a visible pattern is the locked one, while the plain door is the dark one. The patterned door also serves another purpose, which will be discussed later.

At the next corner, a light draws the player forward and encourages them to investigate. During this movement, the player also notices an interaction point on the right, where they learn that the next objective is to find a fuse. Inside the cabinet beneath the lamp, the player can find the key that opens the patterned door encountered earlier.

After unlocking the door with the key, the player enters the room and is immediately presented with the typewriter in the lower right corner, which is used to save progress. One important design point here is the use of color contrast. Most of the room is presented in a bright, pale tone, while the typewriter stands out as a dark object, making it naturally noticeable to the player.

Another important point is that the door pattern mentioned earlier is actually linked to the typewriter. Doors with that pattern lead to rooms containing a typewriter, and the level makes the player unlock this door with a key to reinforce that impression.

On the other side of the room, the player can find the locked fuse. However, the more important discovery at this moment is actually the lighter next to it. Here, the level uses a form of misdirection: the fuse first draws attention as the obvious objective, but the real reward of this exploration is the lighter. Without it, the player would only learn where the fuse is, which could make the progress in this section feel limited. By giving the player the lighter instead, the level turns this encounter into a more meaningful step forward, since it immediately opens up further exploration in both the nearby room and the previously seen dark room. This creates both a sense of surprise and a stronger feeling that the player is still making progress.

In the neighboring room, the player finds a note explaining where the screwdriver needed to unlock the fuse can be found. Shortly after, the player is chased by the boss during exploration. In practice, the escape route is relatively fixed, because the boss consistently blocks the player’s forward path. As the player turns and runs back, the first thing they are likely to notice is the room lit by white light shown in the image. This draws them toward it, where they can hide and also learn that the boss is afraid of light.

Here, sightline design is used again to make sure the player notices the white-lit room during the chase, increasing the chance that they will run toward it.

The player then reaches the location mentioned in the note. Although the note already explains where the item can be found, the level still uses lighting and the color of interactive objects to indicate its exact position in the space.

After the player obtains the screwdriver, the boss is triggered to investigate. Whether the player chooses to move stealthily or run directly, reaching the corridor with the earlier red lighting will always trigger the same sequence, in which the boss bursts through the door.

What makes this moment work is the way the level uses the red lighting together with route planning to shape the player’s emotions. When the player first encounters this red-lit corridor, it creates a sense of danger and caution. However, the level then leads the player through this area multiple times without any immediate threat, which gradually lowers the player’s guard. The later ambush works because it finally releases the danger that the red lighting had been suggesting all along.

At the same time, the boss breaking through the door creates a new route, giving the player another way to bypass the boss and reach the fuse.

Conclusion

In conclusion, this section of Resident Evil 9 shows how level design can guide the player not only through space, but also through expectation and emotion. The sequence uses sightline design, color, interaction feedback, and route planning to control what the player notices and how they move through the environment. It also uses repeated exposure and delayed danger to shape tension, turning familiar spaces into new situations.
