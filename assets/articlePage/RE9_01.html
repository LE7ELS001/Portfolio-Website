Resident Evil 9

Level Design Breakdown 01

Overview

This article is a personal analysis of Resident Evil 9’s level design based on my own playthrough. It looks at how color, lighting, and route design are used throughout the level.

Breakdown

The game begins by placing the player in a small, cluttered alley. The level then puts a bright red object at the center of the player’s view to create a focal point. As the player moves, another red object appears, this time with yellow tape around it. This creates a relay between focal points, making the player look toward the next destination.

After progressing a little further, the player sees the yellow tape again. Here, it does more than simply point the way. It starts to establish the idea that yellow tape marks the correct path, setting up this visual cue for later use in the level.

The player then enters the kitchen area. In my view, one weaker point here is that the level uses a rotating fan and lighting to direct the player toward the next target point. However, the previous section has already taught the player to turn on the flashlight, and the player will likely still have it on at this moment. As a result, their attention is more likely to follow the flashlight beam, which reduces the effect of the lighting guidance in the kitchen.

In the following room, the blue-circled area on the left is the player’s final interaction point, where they need to examine a photograph. However, the level first draws the player’s attention elsewhere by using a yellow sign placed on the ground. As the player moves slightly closer, an interaction prompt appears, naturally pulling their attention toward that point. From there, they are likely to notice the yellow caution tape extending behind the sofa, which encourages them to continue moving in that direction. This leads them to discover an open, lit doorway.

The design here works to avoid making the player turn back and check the left side of the room too early. When the player eventually leaves the room without finding anything, they then notice the photograph in the left area. At this moment, the interaction prompt is triggered from a much greater distance, drawing the player toward it.

What makes this design effective is the way it combines observable angles, environmental arrangement, and interaction trigger distance to guide the player through the entire space without making the guidance feel explicit. The player also ends up walking a repeated route. Normally, this kind of unrewarded backtracking could feel frustrating. However, because the key item was actually within reach from the beginning and was missed by the player rather than physically blocked by the level, the repeated path feels less like forced repetition by the designer and more like the result of the player’s own oversight.

Conclusion

In conclusion, this section of Resident Evil 9 demonstrates how level design can direct player attention through color, lighting, scene arrangement, and interaction cues. The level constantly shapes what the player notices and when they notice it, creating a controlled path through the space without relying on direct instruction. As a result, even repeated movement feels integrated into the experience rather than obviously forced.
