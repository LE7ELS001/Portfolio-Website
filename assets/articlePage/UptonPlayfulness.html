How Upton's Playfulness Is Reflected in the Level Design of The Clockwork Mansion

Overview

This article explores how Upton’s concept of playfulness appears in the level design of Dishonored 2’s Clockwork Mansion. By focusing on a stealth sequence in which the player is suddenly pushed from safety into danger, it examines how the level provides multiple meaningful choices with different risks, outcomes, and information, turning the situation into a playful one.

Upton's Concept of Playfulness

According to Upton, playfulness emerges when a game creates a balance between choice, variety, consequence, predictability, uncertainty, and satisfaction. These elements work together to make a situation feel both understandable and open, giving the player room to experiment while still making their decisions meaningful.

Dishonored 2 Gameplay Core Loop

In Dishonored 2’s stealth gameplay, the core loop can be understood as observe, plan, and execute. The player first studies the environment, enemy positions, patrol routes, and possible paths. Based on that information, they decide how to move forward, whether by hiding, using abilities, taking an alternate route, or waiting for a better moment. Finally, they carry out that decision and respond to the result choice.

Selected Gameplay Sequence

The selected sequence takes place near the beginning of the level, where the player’s space changes and they are about to face enemies.

Choice

Choice is a key part of this sequence. Upton argues that a playful situation should give the player enough options to think about, but not so many that it becomes confusing. In this moment, the player has three main choices. First, they can rush through the door before it closes and reach another area. This avoids the enemies that are about to appear, although the player does not know what is waiting ahead. Second, they can hide in the side areas. This is the easiest option, but also the most uncertain, because enemies might patrol there later. Third, they can drop down through the gap in the floor. This is the riskiest option, but it can also lead to an unexpected reward, since the player can find a way to disable the electric wall.

As time passes, the available choices begin to change. The first and third options disappear, and two new options take their place. The fourth option is to hide under the display cases. This is simple to do, but because it is located in the middle of the space, leaving that position later is much riskier. The fifth option is to move to the upper area and stand on the chandelier. This is the most difficult choice to perform, but it gives the player a safe position from which they can observe what happens next.

Throughout the sequence, the level keeps the number of available choices at around two or three. This prevents the situation from becoming either confusing or too limited. At the same time, each option comes with its own risks and advantages, which creates meaningful differences between them and makes the player’s choice feel important.

Variety

Variety in this sequence comes from both a change in the environment and a change in the player’s situation. Elements in the space, such as the display cases, take on a new function by becoming cover that can block enemy sight. At the same time, the player is pushed from a state of safety into one of immediate danger. This shift creates a new situation that requires the player to reassess the space and make fresh decisions, rather than simply repeating the same stealth behavior.

Consequence

Consequence in this sequence comes from the fact that each choice leads to a different situation, rather than simply a different position. Some options only provide temporary safety. For example, the result of choice 2 allows the player to avoid immediate danger, but they soon have to make another decision. Other options provide a safer space for observation, but with different advantages. Choice 4 offers a more limited view, while choice 5 gives the player a wider perspective of the situation. Some choices can also lead to more meaningful outcomes. In the case of choices 1 and 3, the result is the discovery of a new way forward into the next area. These consequences affect the player’s later choices, their level of safety, and their understanding of the space.

Predictability & Uncertainty

Predictability in this sequence mainly comes from the enemies’ patrol routes. As long as they are not alerted, their movement stays consistent, so the player can observe their patterns and plan a stealth route. At the same time, uncertainty remains because the player still cannot fully know whether the plan will work. Even if a route looks possible, it is hard to tell whether the enemy will turn at the right moment, or whether there is enough time to reach the next cover safely.

Satisfaction

According to Upton, play should stay within an acceptable play experience. Earlier, I mentioned that the level keeps the number of choices between boredom and confusion by changing the situation dynamically. However, stealth games often have unstable outcomes, because being spotted by an enemy can already feel like failure. This means that even a one-second difference in timing can create an undesirable outcome. Dishonored 2 deals with this through its built-in quick save and quick load system, using F5 and F9. This lets the player return almost instantly to the start of execution. Because of that, a previous undesirable outcome is no longer just failure, but also becomes part of predictability, since the player now better understands the situation. In this way, the game reduces frustration and keeps the experience within Upton’s acceptable play range.

Conclusion

In conclusion, this sequence from Dishonored 2’s Clockwork Mansion shows how Upton’s idea of playfulness can be expressed through level design. The player is given meaningful choices, different outcomes, and enough information to plan, while uncertainty keeps the situation tense. Even failure does not fully break the experience, because the game’s quick save and quick load system allows the player to return and try again with more knowledge. As a result, the sequence feels playful not because it is easy, but because it turns stealth into a process of observation, experimentation, and repeated decision-making.
