Resident Evil 9

Level Design Breakdown 02

Overview

This article focuses on how the level guides the player through a small interior space by using sightline design, object placement, interaction prompts, lighting, and repeated visual cues. Rather than relying on explicit directions, the level gradually builds the player’s understanding of where to look and where to go.

Breakdown

After entering the door, the player is immediately presented with another doorway ahead. This uses sightline design to ensure that the player can clearly see the area the level intends them to move toward.

After exploring this area, the player moves upstairs. Once again, the level uses sightline design to draw attention toward the door ahead. As the player gets closer, an interaction prompt appears. The door is also marked with a highly visible red exit sign, and because it cannot be opened, this moment leaves a clear impression on the player.

As the player continues looking deeper into the room, the yellow caution tape begins to guide their attention again. The tape placed along the steps creates a sense of forward movement, while the tape in the distance draws the player’s attention toward the door on the left.

However, opening the door requires a key item. When the player turns back to search for it, they can see that the required item is actually placed on the table beside the staircase. Here, the level uses observable angles together with the chairs placed on the table to shape the player’s first impression. When the player initially walks up the stairs, the table can easily read as being occupied only by chairs, which makes it less likely to be searched.

After the player picks up the item and returns to open the door, a mounted deer head suddenly falls from the wall and startles them. What makes this section effective is that it encourages the player to repeatedly move through a very small area in a natural way, without making that repetition feel dull. The player is first led to overlook the key item, then rewarded with the discovery that it was hidden in a previously visited space, and finally surprised by a scripted scare on the return path. Because the repeated movement is supported by discovery and surprise, it feels like a changing experience rather than simple backtracking.

Through a following cutscene, the player is informed that the protagonist’s mother hid something behind the painting. The cutscene also deliberately leaves the player with another visual reference point: the hotel luggage cart.

The player then returns downstairs to look for the painting. On the way, they pass by the same locked door with the sign again, which reinforces the player’s memory of it.

As the player walks downstairs, a thunderclap occurs and the lightning briefly illuminates the scene. This draws the player’s attention to the hotel luggage cart on the right, which had already been established as a reference point in the earlier cutscene. The player is then naturally encouraged to turn the camera in that direction, where they can spot the painting they are looking for. In this short downstairs sequence, the level uses the flash of lightning together with the placement of the luggage cart to guide the player’s viewing direction, allowing the target to be noticed without explicit instruction.

The protagonist is then attacked, and after obtaining the key, the game shifts into an escape sequence. At this moment, the game briefly pauses after the player picks up the key. Besides giving the player a short moment to breathe before the chase begins, this pause also creates a chance to closely observe the key itself. By doing so, the player can notice that the key carries the same symbol as the door they were unable to open earlier, making it clear which direction they should run toward. After the player runs upstairs, the game also turns on the light near the door, allowing the correct path to stand out immediately even in the middle of a tense escape. What makes this design effective is that it combines a game mechanic, the brief pause that encourages the player to inspect the item, with environmental guidance through lighting and the repeated exposure to the marked door earlier. As a result, even under pressure, the player can still find the intended route almost instinctively.

Conclusion

In conclusion, this section of Resident Evil 9 shows how level design can guide the player through a combination of sightline design, environmental arrangement, interaction timing, and lighting. The level repeatedly shapes what the player notices and when they notice it, allowing the player to move through the space in a controlled order without making that control feel obvious. Even when the player is asked to revisit the same area, the experience is supported by discovery, memory, and surprise, which prevents the repeated movement from feeling dull.
